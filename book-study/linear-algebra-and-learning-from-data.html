Main Study Thread: Taylor Expansion

Taylor expansion approximates a complicated function near a known point using local information.

f(x) approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)

First-order Taylor expansion is the tangent-line approximation: same value, same slope.

Example We Studied

For f(x) = x^2 around a = 2:

f(2)=4, f'(2)=4, so x^2 approx 4 + 4(x-2)

At x = 2.1, the exact value is 4.41 and the approximation is 4.4, so the error is only 0.01.

From Taylor To Gradient Descent

Backpropagation later

In one dimension, local change is predicted by:

f(x+h) approx f(x) + f'(x)h

To decrease the function, choose h opposite the sign of f'(x). That gives the gradient descent update:

x_next = x_current - eta * f'(x_current)

Second-Order View

The first derivative gives slope. The second derivative gives curvature: how the slope changes.

f(x) approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + f''(a)(x-a)^2 / 2!

For f(x)=x^2, the second derivative is 2, so the second-order Taylor expansion is exact. This is why quadratic models become important later in Newton's method.

SVD Context

SVD was supporting context in the same study session. The key relation was:

A v_i = sigma_i u_i

Here v_i is a right singular vector, u_i is a left singular vector, and sigma_i is the stretch amount. Both singular vectors are unit vectors, but they do not need to be standard basis vectors.

Main Takeaways

  • Taylor expansion was the central topic of this study update.
  • First-order Taylor expansion is a tangent-line approximation.
  • The derivative predicts local change and gives the direction needed for descent.
  • Second-order Taylor expansion adds curvature through the second derivative.
  • Gradient descent is motivated by choosing a local step that lowers the Taylor approximation.
